## Purpose

This note recovers the boundary-geometry / spectral-boundary grammar as a Heller-Godel framework object.

The recovered idea is not a proof and not a physics result. It is a disciplined grammar for tracking how boundary conditions, symmetry, quotienting, admissible modes, spectra, periodic orbits, recurrence, and revival-like behavior can be discussed across mathematical and physical research lanes without collapsing them into theorem-grade claims.

The note exists to prevent loss and category drift. It gives future work a shared vocabulary while preserving distance boundaries.

## Core grammar

The working grammar is:

```text
boundary condition
-> admissible configuration space
-> symmetry / quotient rule
-> eigenbasis or mode family
-> spectral data
-> periodic orbit / recurrence structure
-> revival or reconstruction behavior
-> admissible interpretation under claim-grade controls
```

Each arrow is a typed transition, not an automatic proof transfer.

## Motivating examples

### Polygonal billiards and folded symmetry

Polygonal billiards provide a concrete model where boundary geometry constrains allowed trajectories, unfolding turns reflection into translation on a covering surface, and periodicity/recurrence becomes analyzable through the unfolded geometry.

In earlier discussion, equilateral triangle billiards were useful because symmetry, folding, quotienting, orbit closure, and spectral arithmetic can be seen cleanly. In this note, that example functions only as a grammar seed.

No claim is made that triangle billiards prove any Heller-Godel, Yang-Mills, Hodge, or time-theory result.

### Boundary-to-spectrum discipline

Boundary conditions determine which modes are admissible. Once an admissible mode family exists, spectral data can be studied, but spectral data does not by itself license a physical or proof-theoretic conclusion.

The claim boundary is:

```text
mode admissibility is not proof of interpretation
spectral structure is not proof of theorem relevance
recurrence is not proof of causal or physical recurrence
revival analogy is not proof of reconstruction
```

### Wythoff / Schwarz finite-generative syntax

Wythoff and Schwarz-style symbolic descriptions can serve as compact syntax for finite generative rules under symmetry constraints.

Within this framework, they are treated as grammar for admissible construction, not as proof of a downstream mathematical claim.

Possible use:

```text
finite reflection / symmetry syntax
-> allowed construction family
-> generated chamber / tessellation / quotient object
-> candidate boundary grammar
```

Distance boundary: vocabulary and generative syntax only, unless later promoted by a proof-bearing domain artifact.

## Framework objects

### Boundary condition

A rule constraining admissible states, trajectories, fields, proofs, operators, or configurations at a boundary.

Boundary conditions may be geometric, analytic, algebraic, categorical, computational, or governance-like. Cross-domain use must declare analogy distance.

### Admissible configuration space

The space of configurations that satisfy the boundary condition. This may be a geometric domain, quotient, moduli candidate, proof-family domain, operator domain, or state space.

### Symmetry / quotient rule

A rule identifying configurations under a symmetry, reflection, group action, covering map, folding, gluing, or quotient construction.

The quotient rule controls what counts as the same configuration and what information is lost or preserved.

### Eigenbasis / mode family

A family of admissible modes, functions, characters, proof families, operators, or basis-like objects indexed by the configuration space and boundary rule.

Mode existence does not imply completeness, convergence, physical meaning, or proof relevance unless separately shown.

### Spectral data

Eigenvalues, frequency data, phase data, growth rates, singular exponents, characters, recurrence periods, or other structured invariants extracted from the mode family.

Spectral data must remain bound to its construction and claim grade.

### Periodic orbit / recurrence structure

A repeated or approximately repeated structure produced by the boundary/symmetry dynamics.

Recurrence may be exact, asymptotic, statistical, combinatorial, or only analogical. The type must be declared.

### Revival / reconstruction behavior

A later state, projection, or observable recovers information from earlier boundary-governed spectral data.

This is a high-risk analogy term. It must not be used as a physics claim, time-travel claim, cognitive claim, or proof-transfer claim without separate domain evidence.

## Distance controls

Every use of this grammar must declare one of:

- `vocabulary`: terms only;
- `method-grade analogy`: structure suggests a way to organize investigation;
- `diagnostic`: computational or empirical check inside a pinned protocol;
- `lemma-grade`: local proved result inside a domain repo;
- `theorem-grade`: only after formal proof and review.

Default distance is `vocabulary`.

## Anti-seed entries

### A-HG-BSG-001 — Boundary analogy is not theorem evidence

A boundary/spectral analogy does not support a theorem claim unless a domain-specific proof or pinned diagnostic protocol supplies evidence.

### A-HG-BSG-002 — Spectral resemblance is not proof transfer

Similarity between spectra, modes, recurrence behavior, or quotient structures does not transfer proof obligations across domains.

### A-HG-BSG-003 — Revival language is high-risk

Revival/reconstruction language must not be used to imply physical recurrence, cognitive recognition, causal closure, or time-theory claims without separate domain evidence.

### A-HG-BSG-004 — Finite-generative syntax is not completeness

A Wythoff/Schwarz-style syntax can generate a family of objects, but this does not prove that the family is complete, canonical, or relevant to a target theorem.

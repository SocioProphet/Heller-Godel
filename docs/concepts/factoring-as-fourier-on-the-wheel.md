# Factoring as Fourier-on-the-Wheel

This explainer records the intuition behind HG-MTH-008.5 without promoting it beyond the standard theorem boundary. The point is not that factoring settles P vs NP. The point is that factoring has a hidden abelian symmetry, and Fourier analysis is exactly the operation that exposes that symmetry.

## 1. The two maps

Multiplication is easy in the forward direction: given p and q, computing `n = p*q` is polynomial-time. Factoring asks for the inverse direction: given n, recover p and q. This is the cleanest arithmetic instance of the verify-vs-search gap abstracted by P vs NP.

A proposed factorization is easy to verify. Multiply the proposed factors and test primality when needed. Agrawal, Kayal, and Saxena proved primality is in P, so the certificate side is classical and efficient. The search side remains the difficult part.

## 2. Reduce factoring to finding a period

Shor's reduction turns factoring into order-finding. Pick a base `a` with `gcd(a,n)=1`, and look at

```text
a, a^2, a^3, ... mod n
```

Because `a` is a unit modulo n, these powers live in the wheel

```text
G_n = (Z/nZ)^x.
```

Eventually the sequence repeats. The period is

```text
r = ord_n(a) = min { r > 0 : a^r = 1 mod n }.
```

If r is even and `a^(r/2) != -1 mod n`, then

```text
gcd(a^(r/2)-1, n), gcd(a^(r/2)+1, n)
```

produce nontrivial factors. Bad choices of a happen; the standard reduction handles them by retrying.

So factoring becomes period-finding on the wheel.

## 3. A period is a hidden symmetry

The hiding function is

```text
f_a(x) = a^x mod n.
```

It is constant on cosets of the subgroup `rZ <= Z`. That is the abelian Hidden Subgroup Problem: recover the hidden subgroup from a function that is constant on its cosets and separates different cosets.

In this case, finding the subgroup `rZ` is the same as finding the order r.

## 4. Fourier reads the symmetry

For a finite abelian group G with hidden subgroup H, the Fourier transform concentrates on the annihilator

```text
H^perp = { chi in G^ : chi|_H = 1 }.
```

The annihilator is the dual-space shadow of the hidden symmetry. Sampling enough of it recovers H by duality.

In the concrete Shor setting, one works over a finite cyclic sampling window `Z/QZ`. The DFT/QFT of a period-r comb has peaks at multiples of `Q/r` when `r | Q`. When `r` does not divide `Q`, the peaks leak into nearby frequencies, and continued fractions recover r from measured values close to `kQ/r`.

This is “Fourier-on-the-wheel”: the character decomposition of the cyclic subgroup generated by a is the period extractor.

## 5. The measure space

The conversational phrase “they are equal only in the same measure space” becomes precise here. Verify and search become adjoint only after passing to the Plancherel dual. The move is

```text
H <-> H^perp.
```

The hidden period H and the sampled character support `H^perp` are dual descriptions of the same symmetry. The Plancherel-normalized dual is the measure space where the relationship is exact.

## 6. Classical vs quantum

| Step | Classical cost | Quantum (Shor) |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| Evaluate `f_a(x) = a^x mod n` | polynomial by fast exponentiation | polynomial |
| Find period r | **no known polynomial algorithm**; this is the hard step | polynomial via QFT |
| Recover factor from r | polynomial by gcd post-processing | polynomial |

The repository implementation is a classical DFT demonstration. It proves the mechanism on finite examples. It does not provide a fast classical period-finder, and it does not alter the known complexity status of factoring.

## 7. Where it stops

Fourier-on-the-wheel solves the abelian case, which is exactly the easy case; the difficulty of P vs NP lives in the non-abelian world this construction does not enter.

Abelian HSP covers factoring and discrete logarithm. That is why Shor's algorithm works. It is also why this material does not settle P vs NP. Factoring is in NP ∩ coNP and is not believed to be NP-complete.

The hard frontier includes non-abelian HSP settings such as Graph Isomorphism over symmetric groups and dihedral HSP connections to lattice problems. Those are explicitly outside this claim.

## 8. What this does not prove

This does not prove P = NP or P != NP. It does not prove factoring is outside P. It does not give a fast classical factoring algorithm. It does not extend the wheel/Fourier picture to non-abelian HSP. It does not use the Logos, Tannakian, or cultural-projection synthesis tier.
